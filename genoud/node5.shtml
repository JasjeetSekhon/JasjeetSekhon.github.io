Conclusion

Whether or not they are explicitly nonlinear, many empirical models used in the social sciences have nonlinear estimating equations. If one wishes to use these models, significant optimization difficulties may need to be overcome. For example, the discrepancy functions of all of the following models are not in general globally concave: ARIMA models (Box and Jenkins 1976), censored or truncated data models (Maddala 1983; Amemiya 1985), covariance structure models (Bollen 1989), duration models (Heckman and Singer 1985; Lancaster 1990), endogenous switching models (Maddala 1983), generalized linear models (McCullagh and Nelder 1992) and systems based on generalized linear models (Fahrmeir and Tutz 1994), Markov models (Amemiya 1985), multinomial probit choice models (Maddala 1983) and nonlinear simultaneous equation models (Gallant 1987). For such models, optimization methods driven entirely by derivative and therefore local information cannot generally be considered reliable.

In our Monte Carlo experiments even an enhanced derivative based algorithm, the EBFGS, has an error rate as high as 88.7% while GENOUD's error rate is always below 1%. In one problem, the Discrete Comb Density, the EBFGS's error rate ranges from 23.1% to 88.7% while GENOUD's error rate ranges from 0.0% to 0.4%. Too often users may not try more than a few distinct starting values in part because derivative based algorithms are not optimized for a large number of such starts. Therefore, in practice, derivative based systems probably perform even worse than one would infer from our experimental results.

Notwithstanding the power of our optimizer, the user must still apply good judgment based on understanding of the mathematical properties of the objective function, the substantive meaning and statistical properties of the model, and the form and content of the data. As the four-dimensional Hopf model example illustrates, there may be important tradeoffs to consider between local hill-climbing efficiency and global optimization. We hope that the availability of an effective, efficient and simple to use program for nonlinear optimization will encourage the use of nonlinear models in the social sciences.

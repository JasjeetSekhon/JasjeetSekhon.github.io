Application: Domains of Economic Assessment

Kinder, Adams and Gronke [KAG] (1989) attempt to show that person, group and nation constitute related but distinct domains of economic assessment. In trying to test this claim they settle on a LISREL model for 15 observed variables. KAG's LISREL model has four latent common factors: 1, personal economic assessments; 2, personal economic experiences; 3, group economic assessments; and 4, national economic assessments. The model has the factor variances fixed at the value 1.0 and all four factors freely correlated with one another, and includes six free covariances among the observed variables' unique disturbances (i.e., is not diagonal). Many of the observed variables have nonzero sample skewness values or sample kurtosis values different from 3, which strongly suggests that the joint distribution of the observed variables is not multivariate normal. The large value of Mardia's multivariate kurtosis statistic (43.149) further supports such an impression.

According to the usual normal-theory test, KAG's model does not fit the data. According to KAG (1989, 500), their model has goodness-of-fit statistic with df=75. But the critical value for the normal-theory test of goodness-of-fit for 75 degrees of freedom is 96.22. We were not able to replicate KAG's results exactly, mostly because we were unable to obtain exactly the same subsample of the 1984 ANES data, but we came very close. We have sample size n=825, while KAG had n=851. Our parameter estimates and estimated goodness-of-fit statistics are similar to theirs. Experimental results suggest that the bootstrapped LR test is robust against both skewness and excess kurtosis (Mebane, Sekhon and Wells 1996).

A difficulty with KAG's model from the perspective of their theoretical interests is the correlated errors they include among variables that refer to different domains. The model fits the data only when parameters are introduced that represent relationships between observed variables from different domains that are not mediated through their domain-defining latent variables. The model includes correlated errors among the national, group and personal inflation variables, and between the group and national employment variables. The covariances among the errors for the inflation variables suggests that there may be at least one cross-domain economic assessment latent variable, pertaining to inflation and perhaps employment. The free loading of the group employment variable on the ``national domain'' latent variable (parameter ) is also questionable from the point of view of domain separation.

To test the possible existence of a domain-crossing latent factor associated with concerns about inflation or prices, we specify an alternative model that removes all the cross-domain correlated errors but adds a fifth common factor on which loads three observed variables that refer to income and inflation or to ``the cost of things,'' as well as the variable that measures general assessment of the national economic situation. Bootstrapping by reestimating both specifications in each of 1000 resamples for the original sample and computing in each resample gives no statistical reason to prefer one specification to the other.
